The Core Value Proposition
Q = m × Lf

Day: PCM melts at 23–27°C → absorbs heat → interior stays cool
Night: PCM solidifies → releases stored heat → interior stays warm

04 — Sustainable Design

Four innovations.
One greener brick.

Our redesign targets the four biggest inefficiencies in current PCM brick production — replacing petroleum feedstocks, recovering waste heat, closing material loops, and cutting water waste.

🌴

Bio-Based PCM Replacement

Replace paraffin wax with coconut fatty acids or palm-based stearic acid. Same latent heat range (150–220 kJ/kg), renewable feedstock, biodegradable at end-of-life. Melt point tunable by blending fatty acid ratios to precisely hit 22–28°C comfort range.

♨️

Kiln Waste Heat Recovery

Install a heat exchanger on kiln flue gas exhaust to preheat incoming combustion air. Recovers ~25–30% of flue gas enthalpy — approximately 900–1,200 MJ/tonne saved per production run. Reduces external fuel input and CO₂ emissions proportionally.

🏗️

Recycled Aggregate Substitution

Replace 20–30% of virgin clay input with fly ash (coal combustion byproduct) or recycled concrete aggregate. Fly ash has pozzolanic binding properties — maintains structural strength while diverting industrial waste from landfill and cutting raw material cost.

💧

Closed-Loop Water Recycling

Install settling tanks and filtration to recycle process water in clay mixing and washing stages. Target: 40% reduction in freshwater consumption. Reduces wastewater discharge and lowers operating costs — especially valuable in arid manufacturing regions.

Thermodynamic Justification

Each design decision is grounded in quantifiable thermodynamic reasoning — improvements measurable in energy, mass, and entropy terms.

Q = m × Lf
Bio-PCMs with higher latent heat (up to 220 kJ/kg) store more energy per unit mass — reducing PCM loading needed per brick by ~10%, cutting material cost and weight.
ΔS = ΔH / Tm
Entropy change during phase transition is quantifiable and directly linked to thermodynamic efficiency. Process integration reduces irreversibilities — lower entropy generation = higher efficiency.
η = Qrec / Qflue
Heat exchanger recovery efficiency of ~25–30%. Recovering ~1,100 MJ/t from flue gas directly reduces external fuel input — measurable CO₂ reduction per tonne of brick produced.
Conventional Design
  • Petroleum paraffin PCM — non-renewable, price-volatile
  • No kiln heat recovery — ~45% energy wasted as flue gas
  • 100% virgin clay — no recycled content
  • No water recycling loop
  • ~0.28 kg CO₂ per kg brick produced
Our Sustainable Design
  • Bio-based PCM (coconut/stearic acid) — renewable feedstock
  • Heat exchanger on flue gas → 25–30% energy recovered
  • 20–30% fly ash / recycled aggregate substitution
  • Closed-loop water recycling — 40% freshwater reduction
  • Target: ~0.15 kg CO₂ per kg brick (−46% vs. conventional)

07 — Technical Challenges

Honest about
the hard parts.

Every innovative design has real engineering challenges. Here are the four key barriers to PCM brick adoption — and how our design addresses each one.

💧

PCM Leakage Risk

Direct impregnation allows molten PCM to migrate and leak over repeated thermal cycles — especially at elevated temperatures. Structural integrity and thermal performance both degrade as PCM escapes the brick matrix.

Micro-encapsulation in polymer shells (BASF Micronal approach) seals each PCM droplet. Zero leakage recorded across 1,000+ thermal cycles in peer-reviewed testing.
🏗️

Reduced Compressive Strength

Adding PCM cavities reduces effective cross-sectional area. At 20% PCM loading by weight, compressive strength drops by 10–15% vs. standard brick — a concern for load-bearing wall applications.

Fly ash substitution compensates via pozzolanic reactions. Cavity geometry optimization in SolidWorks minimizes strength loss while maximizing PCM volume per brick.
💸

Higher Upfront Cost

PCM bricks currently cost 1.4–1.8× more than standard bricks. This is a significant barrier in affordable housing markets and developing regions where construction budgets are severely constrained.

Payback period of 3–7 years via HVAC savings. At scale, bio-PCM sourcing and heat recovery reduce manufacturing cost. Green building incentives and carbon credits offset the upfront premium.
🌡️

Low Thermal Conductivity

Paraffin and bio-based PCMs have low thermal conductivity (~0.2 W/m·K) — heat transfer into and out of the PCM is slow, reducing charge/discharge rate and limiting performance in extreme climates.

Expanded graphite or metal foam inserts inside PCM cavities boost effective thermal conductivity by 5–10×. Trade-off against cost and mass is optimized for target climate zone.
